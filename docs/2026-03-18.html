💡 CONCEPT REFRESHER

Today's concept: Perplexity

Perplexity
Perplexity measures how well a probability model predicts a sample — formally it's the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood per token: PPL = exp(-(1/N) * Σ log P(xᵢ | x. Intuitively, a perplexity of K means the model is as uncertain as if it were choosing uniformly among K options at each token. Lower perplexity means the model assigns higher probability to the actual next tokens, indicating better predictive fit on that distribution. In practice, perplexity is heavily dataset-dependent: a model with low perplexity on web text may have high perplexity on legal contracts, so cross-domain perplexity comparisons are unreliable without controlling for the evaluation corpus. A common misconception is that lower perplexity on a held-out benchmark directly implies better downstream task performance — in reality, perplexity and task accuracy can diverge significantly, especially after instruction tuning or RLHF.

Why can't you directly compare perplexity scores across two models evaluated on different datasets?

Perplexity is a function of both model quality and dataset difficulty; a lower score on dataset A vs. dataset B tells you nothing about relative model quality.

After RLHF fine-tuning, a model's perplexity on a standard language modeling benchmark often increases — why?

RLHF optimizes for human preference signals, not next-token likelihood, so the model's probability distribution shifts away from the original corpus distribution, raising perplexity even as task quality improves.

How would you use perplexity as a signal in a RAG pipeline?

High perplexity on a generated answer conditioned on retrieved context can flag low-confidence or poorly-grounded outputs for review, acting as a cheap uncertainty proxy before a more expensive verification step.
