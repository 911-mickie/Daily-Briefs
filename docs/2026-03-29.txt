• <b><a href="https://machinelearning.apple.com/research/less-gaussians-texture-more">Less Gaussians, Texture More: 4K Feed-Forward Textured Splatting</a></b>
Existing feed-forward 3D Gaussian Splatting methods tie primitive count to pixel count, causing quadratic growth that makes 4K synthesis intractable. LGTM decouples this by predicting compact Gaussian primitives with per-primitive textures — geometric complexity no longer scales with rendering resolution. The result is high-fidelity 4K novel view synthesis without per-scene optimization, using significantly fewer primitives than prior methods. This is directly relevant for any production pipeline doing real-time 3D reconstruction or AR/VR rendering at high resolution.
<i>Interview angle:</i> An interviewer might probe the trade-off between primitive count and texture resolution in Gaussian Splatting, and how decoupling geometry from rendering resolution changes scalability characteristics.

• <b><a href="http://bair.berkeley.edu/blog/2026/01/10/information-driven-imaging/">Information-Driven Design of Imaging Systems</a></b>
Traditional imaging system evaluation uses resolution and SNR independently, which fails when systems trade one for the other — and conflates hardware quality with algorithm quality when neural networks are used for evaluation. This BAIR/NeurIPS 2025 work proposes mutual information between measurements and objects as a single unified metric: two systems with equal MI are equivalent in distinguishing objects regardless of how measurements look. Optimizing for MI directly produces imaging system designs that match state-of-the-art end-to-end methods while requiring less memory, less compute, and no task-specific decoder. Directly applicable to multimodal sensor pipelines where you're choosing between hardware configurations.
<i>Interview angle:</i> Mutual information as an evaluation metric is a strong concept to discuss when asked how you'd compare two feature extraction or sensor pipelines without training a full downstream model.

💡 <b>CONCEPT REFRESHER</b>
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

<b>Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)</b>
RAG augments an LLM's response by retrieving relevant documents from an external knowledge store at inference time and injecting them into the prompt context, rather than relying solely on parametric knowledge baked into model weights. The intuition is simple: models have a knowledge cutoff and finite context, but your retrieval index can be updated continuously and queried selectively. In practice, RAG shines when you need factual grounding, attribution, or domain-specific knowledge that changes faster than you can fine-tune. The most common misconception is that better embeddings alone fix retrieval quality — in production, chunking strategy, metadata filtering, reranking, and query rewriting often matter more than the embedding model choice. RAG is not a silver bullet for hallucination either; if the retrieved chunk is wrong or irrelevant, the model will confidently use it.

<b>Q1:</b> What's the difference between sparse retrieval (BM25) and dense retrieval (embedding-based), and when would you use each? → <i>BM25 excels on keyword-heavy or out-of-domain queries with no training data; dense retrieval wins on semantic similarity — hybrid approaches combining both typically outperform either alone in production.</i>

<b>Q2:</b> Why does chunk size matter in RAG, and how do you choose it? → <i>Chunks too large dilute relevance signals and waste context; chunks too small lose surrounding context needed for coherent answers — the right size depends on your query granularity and the embedding model's effective context window.</i>

<b>Q3:</b> How would you evaluate a RAG pipeline end-to-end in production, beyond retrieval recall metrics? → <i>Measure faithfulness (does the answer contradict the retrieved chunk?), answer relevance, and context precision using frameworks like RAGAS, while tracking retrieval latency P95 and fallback rates separately from generation quality.</i>
