💡 CONCEPT REFRESHER

Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Bias measures how far a model's average predictions are from the true values — high bias means the model systematically underfits, missing real patterns regardless of training data. Variance measures how much predictions fluctuate across different training sets — high variance means the model overfits, memorizing noise instead of signal. The tradeoff is structural: reducing bias (more complex model) tends to increase variance, and reducing variance (simpler model, regularization, more data) tends to increase bias. In practice this matters most when choosing model capacity, regularization strength, ensemble strategy, and train/val split size — and it's the theoretical underpinning for why bagging reduces variance while boosting reduces bias. The common misconception is that more data always fixes high variance; it helps, but doesn't eliminate it if model capacity is fundamentally too high relative to the signal-to-noise ratio of the problem.

Why does bagging reduce variance but not bias?

Averaging multiple high-variance models trained on bootstrap samples cancels out their independent noise, but their shared systematic error (bias) persists in the average.

A model has low training loss but high validation loss. Is this a bias or variance problem, and what's your first intervention?

High variance (overfitting) — first intervention is regularization (L2, dropout), then reducing model capacity or increasing training data.

In the context of large pretrained transformers fine-tuned on small datasets, which dominates — bias or variance — and how does this change the regularization strategy?

Variance dominates because the model has vastly more capacity than the fine-tuning signal can constrain; strategies like LoRA, early stopping, and small learning rates act as implicit regularizers to bound effective capacity.
