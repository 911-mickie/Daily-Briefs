💡 CONCEPT REFRESHER

Overfitting and Underfitting
Overfitting occurs when a model learns the training distribution so precisely — including noise and dataset-specific artifacts — that it fails to generalize; the model has high variance and low bias. Underfitting occurs when a model lacks the capacity or training signal to capture the true underlying pattern, yielding high bias and low variance. In practice, this matters most at inflection points: when choosing model size for a new task, deciding when to stop fine-tuning an LLM, or diagnosing why a retrieval ranker with perfect offline metrics degrades in production. A common misconception is that larger models always overfit; modern large models often exhibit double-descent, where performance improves again after an initial overfitting peak as model size or training steps continue to increase. For RAG and embedding systems specifically, overfitting to a synthetic training distribution (e.g., generated QA pairs) is a real risk if the synthetic data doesn't reflect true query diversity.

Q1: What's the practical signal that your fine-tuned embedding model is overfitting to synthetic training data? → Recall improves on held-out synthetic pairs but degrades on real user queries sampled from production logs.
Q2: Why can early stopping be insufficient as an overfitting mitigation for LLM fine-tuning? → Validation loss may not reflect downstream task generalization, especially when the validation set shares artifacts with the training set.
Q3: In the double-descent regime, what does "interpolation threshold" mean and why should a practitioner care? → It's the model size/epoch where training loss hits zero and test loss peaks before improving again — practitioners who stop training here miss the second descent where generalization recovers.
