💡 CONCEPT REFRESHER

Today's concept: Precision, Recall, and F1 Score

Precision, Recall, and F1 Score
Precision is the fraction of positive predictions that are actually correct (TP / (TP + FP)); recall is the fraction of actual positives your model correctly identified (TP / (TP + FN)). They trade off against each other — raising your classification threshold increases precision but lowers recall, and vice versa. F1 is the harmonic mean of the two, useful when you want a single scalar but care about both false positives and false negatives roughly equally. In retrieval systems, precision@K measures result quality while recall@K measures coverage — both matter, which is why NDCG (which accounts for rank order) is often preferred in RAG evaluation. A common misconception is treating F1 as universally appropriate — in heavily imbalanced settings (fraud detection, rare disease diagnosis), a high F1 can still hide catastrophic recall failures on the minority class.

In a RAG pipeline, you retrieve 10 chunks. 7 are relevant. There are 20 total relevant chunks in the corpus. What's your precision@10 and recall@10?

Precision@10 = 0.7; Recall@10 = 7/20 = 0.35.

Why is F1 a harmonic mean rather than arithmetic mean of precision and recall?

The harmonic mean penalizes extreme imbalance — a model with 1.0 precision and 0.0 recall gets F1=0, not 0.5, correctly reflecting it's useless.

You're building a content moderation system where false negatives (missed harmful content) are far more costly than false positives. How do you adjust your optimization target?

Optimize for recall directly (or use F-beta with beta > 1 to weight recall more heavily), and set your classification threshold low enough to accept higher false positive rates in exchange for catching more true positives.
